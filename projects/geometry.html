Geometry Theorems

Basic Facts

BF 1: Through two given points, there is one and only one line.

  • It is possible to draw a line through two points.
  • If two lines have two or more points in common, they must really be the same line.

BF 2: On a ray, there is exactly one point at a given distance from the endpoint.

  • It is possible to find a point on the ray at a given distance from the endpoint.
  • If two points on the ray have the same distance from the endpoint, they must really be the same point.

BF 3: It is possible to extend a line segment to an infinite line.

BF 4: It is possible to find the midpoint of a line segment.

BF 5: It is possible to draw the bisector of an angle.

BF 6: Given a line `l` and a point `P` (which may be either on `l` or not on `l`), it is possible to draw a line through `P` which is perpendicular to `l`.

BF 7: (SSS) If two triangles have three pairs of corresponding sides equal, then the triangles are congruent.

BF 8: (SAS) If two triangles have two pairs of corresponding sides the included angles equal, then the triangles are congruent.

BF 9: (ASA) If two triangles have two pairs of corresponding angles and the included side equal, then the triangles are congruent.

1. Misc

1.1: Vertical angles are congruent.

Prove: `alpha = gamma`

  1. `alpha + beta = 180^circ`

  2. `gamma + beta = 180^circ`

  3. 1,2 `=> alpha = 180^circ - beta, gamma = 180^circ - beta`

  4. 3 `=> alpha = gamma`

1.2: If two sides of a triangle are congruent, then the angles opposite these sides are congruent.

Prove: `angle A = angle B`

  1. At midpoint `M` of `AB`, connect `AM`.

  2. `CM ~= CM`

  3. `CA ~= CB` (given)

  4. `AM ~= BM` (midpoint)

  5. 2,3,4 `=> triangle ACM ~= triangle BCM` (SSS)

  6. `angle B = angle C` (CPCTC)

1.3: If two angles of a triangle are congruent, then the sides opposite them are congruent.

Prove: `AB = AC`

  1. Consider two triangles: `triangle ABC` and `triangle ACB`.

  2. `angle B ~= angle C` (given)

  3. `BC ~= CB`

  4. `angle C ~= angle B` (given)

  5. 2,3,4 `=> triangle ABC ~= triangle ACB` (ASA)

  6. `AB ~= AC` (CPCTC)

1.4: Through a given point outside a given line, there is at most one line perpendicular to the given line.

Prove: Through `P`, there exists only one perpendicular line to line `l`

  1. Let `PB` be the perpendicular line to `l`

  2. Since `PB` is perpendicular to `l`, `angle PCD ~= angle BCD = 90^circ`.

  3. Draw another (unique) line from `P` to `B` (`PAB`). From BF 1 (only one line between two points), this line cannot be a straight line

  4. So `angle PAB` cannot equal `180^circ`

  5. Then `angle PAC` and `angle BAC` both cannot be equal to `90^circ`

  6. Therefore, `PAB` cannot be perpendicular to `l`

2. Triangle Inequalities

2.1: (Exterior Angle Theorem) An exterior angle of a triangle is greater than each interior angle not supplementary to it.

Prove: For `triangle ABC`, `angle BCE > angle A` and `angle BCE > angle B`

  1. Through the midpoint `M` of `BC`, construct `AD` such that `AM ~= DM`

  2. Connect `DC`

  3. `CM ~= BM` (midpoint)

  4. `angle CMD ~= angle BMA` (vertical angles)

  5. `AM ~= DM` (by construction)

  6. 3,4,5 `=> triangle ABM ~= triangle DCM` (SAS)

  7. `angle B ~= angle BCD` (CPCTC)

  8. `angle BCD lt angle BCE`

  9. `angle B lt angle BCE`

(Similar reasoning for `angle A lt angle BCE`)

2.2: In any triangle, the angle opposite to a greater side is greater.

Prove: `AB gt BC => angle C gt angle A`

  1. Since `AB gt BC`, we can construct a line `CD` such that `BD ~= BC`

  2. From 1.2 (angles opposite congruent sides are congruent), `angle BDC ~= angle BCD`

  3. From 2.1 (exterior angle greater than interior angles not supplementary), `angle BDC gt angle A`

  4. Then `angle BCD gt angle A`

  5. So `angle C gt angle BCD gt angle A`

2.3: The side opposite to a greater angle is greater.

Prove: `angle A gt angle C => BC gt AB`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `angle A gt angle C`, but `BC` is not greater than `AB`. Then either `BC ~= AB` or `BC lt AB`

  2. If `BC ~= AB`, then, by 1.2 (angles opposite congruent sides are congruent), `angle A ~= angle C`, which contradicts `angle A gt angle C`

  3. If `BC lt AB`, then, by 2.2 (angle opposite greater side is greater), `angle A lt angle C`, which contradicts `angle A gt angle C`

  4. So `BC gt AB`

2.4 (Triangle Inequality Theorem) In a triangle, each side is smaller than the sum of the other two sides.

(More importantly, the largest side of a triangle is smaller than the sum of the other two smaller sides.)

Prove: `AC lt AB + BC`

  1. Extend `AB` to `AD` such that `BD ~= BC`

  2. Connect `DC`

  3. From 1.2 (angles opposite congruent sides are congruent), `angle D ~= angle DCB`

  4. Since `angle DCB lt angle DCA`, `angle D lt angle DCA`

  5. From 2.3 (side opposite greater angle is greater), `AC lt AB + BD`

  6. Since `BC ~= BD`, `AC lt AB + BC`

2.5 (Hinge Theorem) If two sides of one triangle are congruent respectively to two sides of another triangle, then:

(1) the greater angle contained by these sides is opposed to the greater side;

(2) the greater of the non-congruent sides is opposed to the greater angle.

(1) Prove: `AB ~= A'B', AC ~= A'C', angle A gt angle A' => BC gt B'C`

  1. Overlay `triangle A'B'C'` on `triangle ABC` such that `A'C'` lies exactly on `AC`

  2. Bisect `angle BAB'` with `AD`

  3. `AB ~= A'B'` (given)

  4. `angle BAD ~= angle B'AD` (bisect)

  5. `AD ~= AD`

  6. 3,4,5 `=> triangle ABD ~= triangle AB'D` (SAS)

  7. `BD ~= B'D` (CPCTC)

  8. From 2.4 (side smaller than sum of other two sides), `B'C lt B'D + DC`

  9. Since `B'D ~= BD`, `B'C lt BD + DC`

  10. `BD + DC = BC`, so `B'C lt BC`

(2) Prove: `AB ~= A'B', AC ~= A'C', B'C' gt BC => angle A' gt angle A`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `angle A'` is not greater than `angle A`. Then either `angle A' ~= angle A` or `angle A' lt angle A`

  2. If `angle A' ~= angle A`, then `triangle ABC ~= triangle A'B'C'` (SAS)

  3. If `angle A' lt angle A`, then, by (1), `B'C' lt BC`, which contradicts `B'C' gt BC`

  4. So `angle A' gt angle A`

3. Right Triangles

3.1: If a perpendicular and some slants are drawn to a line from the same point outside this line, then:

(1) if the feet of the slants are the same distance away from the foot of the perpendicular, then such slants are congruent;

(2) if the feet of the slants are not the same distance away from the foot of the perpendicular, then the slant whose foot is farther away from the foot of the perpendicular is greater.

(1) Prove: `BM ~= CM => AB ~= AC`

  1. `BM ~= CM` (given)

  2. `angle AMB ~= angle AMC` (right angles)

  3. `AM ~= AM`

  4. 1,2,3 `=> triangle AMB ~= triangle AMC` (SAS)

  5. `AB ~= AC` (CPCTC)

(2) Prove: `BE gt BC => AE gt AC`

  1. Since `BE gt BC`, we can construct a line `BD` such that `BC ~= BD`

  2. Connect `AD`

  3. From (1), `AD ~= AC`

  4. From 1.2 (angles opposite congruent sides are congruent), `angle C ~= angle ADB`

  5. Since `angle ADB` is exterior to `angle E`, from 2.1 (exterior angle greater than interior angles not supplementary), `angle ADB gt angle E`

  6. 4 `=> angle C gt angle E`

  7. From 2.5.1 (greater angle opposed to greater side), `AE gt AC`

3.2: If a perpendicular and some slants are drawn to a line from the same point outside this line, then:

(1) if two slants are congruent, then their feet are the same distance away from the foot of the perpendicular;

(2) if two slants are not congruent, then the foot of the greater one is farther away from the foot of the perpendicular.

(1) Prove: `BA ~= BC => AM ~= CM`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `AM` is not equal to `CM`. Without loss of generality, let `AM gt CM`

  2. From 3.1.2, `BA gt BC`, which contradicts `BA ~= BC`

(2) Prove: `BC gt BA => CD gt AD`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `CD` is not greater than `AD`. Then either `CD ~= AD` or `CD lt AD`

  2. If `CD ~= AD`, then, by 3.1.1, `BC ~= BA`, which contradicts `BC gt BA`

  3. If `CD lt AD`, then, by 3.1.2, `BC lt BA`, which contradicts `BC gt BA`

3.3: Two right triangles are congruent:

(1) if the hypotenuse and an acute angle of one triangle are congruent to respectively the hypotenuse and an acute angle of the other;

(2) if the hypotenuse and a leg of one triangle are congruent respectively to the hypotenuse and a leg of the other.

(1) Prove: `AB ~= A'B', angle A ~= angle A' => triangle ABC ~= triangle A'B'C'`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `triangle ABC` is not congruent to `triangle A'B'C'`

  2. Overlay `triangle A'B'C'` on `triangle ABC` such that `A'B'` lies exactly on `AB` and `angle A'` lies exactly on `angle A`

  3. Since `triangle ABC` is not congruent with `triangle A'B'C'`, `C'` will either be on the left of `C` (denoted `C'`) or on the right of `C` (denoted `C''`)

  4. Then there will be two perpendiculars from the same point to the same line, which contradicts 1.4 (only one perpendicular through a point)

(2) Prove: `AB ~= A'B', BC ~= B'C' => triangle ABC ~= triangle A'B'C'`

  1. Suppose, for a contradiction, that `triangle ABC` is not congruent to `triangle A'B'C'`

  2. Overlay `triangle A'B'C'` on `triangle ABC` such that `B'C'` lies exactly on `BC`

  3. Since `triangle ABC` is not congruent with `triangle A'B'C'`, `A'` will either be on the left of `A` (denoted `A'`) or on the right of `A` (denoted `A''`)

  4. Then we have congruent slants whose feet are not the same distance away from the foot of the perpendicular, which contradicts 3.2.1 (if two slants are congruent, then their feet are the same distance away from the foot of the perpendicular)

4. Perpendicular Bisectors

4.1: If a point lies on the perpendicular bisector of a segment, then the point is the same distance away from the endpoints of the segment.

Prove: `AB ~= AC`

  1. `BM ~= CM` (bisector)

  2. `angle AMB ~= angle AMC` (right angles)

  3. `AM ~= AM`

  4. 1,2,3 `=> triangle AMB ~= triangle AMC` (SAS)

  5. `AB ~= AC` (CPCTC)

(Can also use 3.1.2 since feet of slants are same distance away from perpendicular)

4.2: If a point is the same distance away from the endpoints of a segment, then the point lies on the perpendicular bisector of the segment.

Prove: `AM ~= CM`

  1. `AB ~= BC` (given)

  2. `BM ~= BM`

  3. From 3.3.2 (hypotenuse,leg of right triangle), `triangle ABM ~= triangle CBM`

  4. `AM ~= CM` (CPCTC)

5. Geometric Constructions

5.1: Construct a triangle with the given three sides `a`, `b`, and `c`.

5.2: Construct an angle congruent to the given angle `ABC` and such that one of the sides is a given line `MN`, and the vertex is at a point `O` given on the line.

5.3: Bisect a given angle.

5.4: Erect a perpendicular to the line `AB` from a given point `C`.

5.5: Drop a perpendicular to a given line `BC` from a given point `A`.

5.6: Draw a perpendicular to a given segment `AB` through its midpoint.

5.7: Bisect a given straight segment.

5.8: Construct a triangle with a given base `b`, an angle `alpha` at the base, and the sum `s` of the other two sides.

6. Parallel Lines

6.1: Two perpendiculars to the same line cannot intersect.

6.2: When two lines are intersected by a third line, and it turns out that:

(1) some corresponding angles are congruent, or

(2) some alternate angles are congruent, or

(3) the sum of some same-side interior angles is `180^circ`,

then these two lines are parallel.

6.3: Through a given point outside a line, there exists a line parallel to the given line.

The parallel postulate: Through a given point, one cannot draw two different lines parallel to the same line.

6.4: If two parallel lines are intersected by any line, then:

(1) corresponding angles are congruent;

(2) alternate angles are congruent;

(3) the sum of same-side interior angles is `180^circ`.

7. Angle Sums of Polygons

7.1: The sum of angles of a triangle is `180^circ`.

7.2: The sum of angles of a convex polygon having `n` sides is `180(n-2)^circ`.

7.3: If at each vertex of a convex polygon, we extend one of the sides of this angle, then the sum of the exterior angles thus formed is `360^circ`.

8. Parallelograms and Trapezoids

Parallelogram: a quadrilateral in which each of its opposite sides are parallel

Rectangle: a parallelogram with one `90^circ` angle

Rhombus: a parallelogram in which 2 adjacent sides are equal

Square: a rhombus with one `90^circ` angle

Trapezoid: a quadrilateral in which one pair of opposite sides are parallel and the second pair of opposite sides is not parallel

8.1: A quadrilateral is a parallelogram if and only if one pair of its opposite sides are parallel and congruent.

8.2: A quadrilateral is a parallelogram if and only if each pair of its opposite angles are equal.

8.3: A quadrilateral is a parallelogram if and only if its diagonals bisect each other.

8.4: A parallelogram is a rectangle if and only if its diagonals are equal.

8.5: A parallelogram is a rhombus if and only if its diagonals are perpendicular.

8.6: A parallelogram is a rhombus if and only if one of its diagonals bisect one of its angles.

8.7: A rhombus is a square if and only if its diagonals are congruent.

8.8: (Midline Theorem for Triangles) The line connecting the midpoints of two sides of a triangle (midline) is parallel to the third segment and equal half of its length.

8.9: A segment emanating from the midpoint of one side of a triangle and parallel to the second must bisect the third.

8.10: The midline of a trapezoid is parallel to the bases and equal half the sum of their lengths.

8.11: A segment emanating from the midpoint of one side of a trapezoid and parallel to the bases must bisect the second side.

8.12: A trapezoid is isosceles if and only if its base angles are congruent.

8.13: A trapezoid is isosceles if and only if its diagonals are congruent.

9. Circles

9.1: Through any three points, not lying on the same line, it is possible to draw one and only one circle.

9.2: The diameter is perpendicular to the chord if and only if it bisects the chord.

9.3: In a circle, two chords are of equal length if and only if they are equidistant from the center of the circle.

9.4: The greater one of two non-congruent chords is closer to the center of the circle.

9.5: A line is tangent to a circle if and only if it is perpendicular to the radius of the circle at the tangency point.

9.6: In a circle, an inscribed angle is half of the arc it subtends; i.e., half of the central angle.

9.7: The angle formed by a chord and a tangent is equal ot any inscribed angle that subtends the chord.

Prove: `angle BAD ~= angle C`

  1. Since `AD` is tangent to circle `O`, `AD bot OA => angle OAD = 90^circ`

  2. 1 `=> angle OAB = 90^circ - angle BAD`

  3. `OA ~= OB` (radii in same circle)

  4. From 1.2 (angles opposite congruent sides are congruent), 3 `=> angle OAB ~= angle OBA`

  5. 2,4 `=> angle OBA = 90^circ - angle BAD`

  6. From 7.1 (sum of angles in triangle is `180^circ`), `angle O = 180^circ - (90^circ - angle BAD) - (90^circ - angle BAD) = 2angleBAD`

  7. From 9.6, `angle C = (2angleBAD)/2 = angle BAD`
